Vietnam’s Growth by DecreeReading the Government’s Roadmap to Industrial Transformation

The opportunity is not in the market data, it is in the government instruments. This briefing examines what they reveal.

An informational briefing prepared by Ocean Bridge Advisory for boards and senior executives evaluating Vietnam as a market, manufacturing base or investment destination.

Executive Summary

Since mid-2022, Vietnam's government has issued a series of resolutions, decrees, laws and development plans that collectively describe how it intends to reshape the economy over the next decade. Taken individually, these instruments address separate topics. Taken together, they form an increasingly integrated industrial strategy, and an unusually transparent map of where the government intends to direct capital, infrastructure, incentives and political attention.

This briefing examines the major instruments issued between 2022 and 2026 and asks four questions of each: what strategic objective is being pursued; how the government intends to achieve it; what commercial opportunities it creates; and how likely delivery is.

  • The vehicle market is at the beginning of its growth curve. Only 9% of Vietnamese households own a car, against 89.4% owning a motorbike. The government's automotive strategy targets roughly double today's car-sales volume by 2030, with the transition electrified from the outset.2827
  • The supplier base is the most tangible commercial opening. Fewer than 100 Tier-1 automotive suppliers serve a market that imports around 80% of its components, and the government has legislated cash support of up to 50% of qualifying investment cost for manufacturers who move to close that gap.3332
  • Incentives have shifted from tax holidays to direct cash. With the OECD global minimum tax neutralising traditional concessions, semiconductor and AI R&D now qualifies for cash grants of up to 50% of initial investment.9
  • The technology sector runs in parallel to automotive. Vietnam has attracted $11.6 billion of registered semiconductor investment, the world’s first standalone Digital Technology Industry Law came into force in January 2026, and the government targets semiconductor revenue above $25 billion by 2030. The same cash incentives available to EV-component makers apply equally to automotive-grade semiconductors and AI R&D.1810
  • Power is the binding constraint. Every electrification and data-centre ambition depends on delivering roughly 90 to 100 gigawatts of grid capacity by 2030. The plan exists; the grid is already congested and major projects run late.37

Investors should treat Vietnam's published targets as indicators of policy direction rather than forecasts of outcome. The country's industrial ambitions are credible. The timing and magnitude of delivery remain considerably less certain.

Why these instruments matter

Many analyses of Vietnam begin with GDP growth, demographics or labour costs. Those factors matter. They do not, however, explain what is happening today.

The defining characteristic of Vietnam's current development model is the degree to which economic transformation is being directed through formal government instruments. The state is not simply setting regulatory conditions and allowing markets to operate. It is publishing specific objectives, attaching incentives and budget, setting timelines, and expecting industry to align.

The instruments examined in this briefing are not independent reforms. The green-transport mandate established the framework for electrification. Supporting-industry legislation created incentives for the supply chain required to support that transition. The national semiconductor strategy set objectives for the technology capability required by modern vehicles and advanced manufacturing. The revised National Power Plan established the infrastructure programme needed to sustain industrial expansion. The International Financial Centre framework opened a new channel for international capital. Each instrument supports the others. The ambition is increasingly integrated, even when the drafting is not.

This matters to foreign executives because it means Vietnam has published more of its economic roadmap than most emerging markets do. The uncertainty is not about direction. It is about delivery.

Why Vietnam, and why now

Vietnam's current momentum is not solely a domestic story. It is occurring at the intersection of several global shifts that are simultaneously creating pressure on foreign companies to act.

Supply chains continue to diversify beyond China. Technology restrictions and geopolitical competition are encouraging manufacturing relocation. Companies are reassessing concentration and resilience risk. ASEAN economies are competing aggressively for industrial investment, and Vietnam is competing harder than most.

The government's policy response has been to position Vietnam as a preferred destination for capital and capability that might otherwise flow elsewhere in Asia. The instruments analysed in this briefing are therefore not only domestic reforms. They are competitive tools designed to attract investment that is already in motion.

The long-term opportunity is not simply that Vietnam will consume more vehicles, electronics or technology products. The more significant opportunity is that Vietnam intends to manufacture them, and is systematically creating the conditions to do so.

The central question

The question facing investors is not whether Vietnam's ambitions are real. The government has stated them clearly and repeatedly, in binding instruments, with named targets and published timelines.

The more important question is whether the country's infrastructure, workforce, institutions and execution capability can deliver at the pace envisioned. History suggests caution. Vietnam has frequently missed industrial targets. Automotive localisation remains well below previous objectives. Supplier development has progressed more slowly than planned. Infrastructure delivery often lags ambition. Yet history also shows that Vietnam rarely abandons strategic priorities once formally adopted. Targets change; timelines slip; the direction generally holds.

For investors and senior executives, that distinction is critical. The instruments in this briefing should not be read as forecasts. They should be read as signals.

02 · The structural resetThe rules a foreign entrant now plays under

The first shift rewrote the state itself. Central ministries were cut from 18 to 14, the Ministry of Planning and Investment was folded into the Ministry of Finance, which now owns investment licensing, and provinces were consolidated from 63 to 34 from July 2025.34

The second shift reset the US trade relationship. A threatened 46% reciprocal tariff settled at 20% from August 2025, with a 40% penalty on transshipped goods and Vietnam agreeing to accept, for sale in its domestic market, vehicles built to US motor vehicle safety and emissions standards, a non-tariff barrier that had previously prevented American-standard vehicles from entering the Vietnamese showroom.5 The transshipment penalty makes genuine local content a board-level compliance matter, and points in the same direction as the localisation policy in Section 04: build, do not badge.

The third shift addressed capital access. Resolution 222/2025 established the Vietnam International Financial Centre, launched December 2025, across Ho Chi Minh City and Da Nang, as a ring-fenced zone operating under international norms even where these diverge from domestic Vietnamese law: relaxed foreign-exchange controls, a dedicated commercial court and international arbitration centre, and a digital-asset sandbox.36 Its regional-hub-by-2035 ambition is aspirational; its near-term value to a foreign executive is more specific.

Key Policy Shift  ·  VIFC

Foreign companies can raise, hold and move capital through a jurisdiction operating under international norms within Vietnam’s borders, without the foreign-exchange restrictions and approval processes that apply in the domestic system. For a company structuring a joint venture, acquisition or capital repatriation, this is a materially different operating environment from what Vietnam has historically offered.

03 · Opening oneThe vehicle market: early on four wheels, electrifying on two

Vietnam is one of the most under-motorised large economies in Asia on four wheels, and one of the most saturated on two. That asymmetry is the opportunity. Ministry of Industry and Trade figures put passenger-car ownership at about 30 per 1,000 people; the 2024 census records 89.4% of households owning a motorbike against just 9% owning a car.2728 Against regional peers the headroom is stark, and so is the gap Vietnam is trying to close.

Four wheels: the decreed target, and the affordability caveat

The clearest official statement of the car opportunity is the Ministry of Industry and Trade's draft Automotive Industry Development Strategy. It remains a draft in consultation, and its headline figure was already cut between versions: from 1 to 1.1 million car sales a year by 2030 in the 2024 draft to roughly 800,000 to 900,000 by 2030 in the December 2025 draft. Either way it implies roughly doubling the market against the 2024 actual of about 494,300 units.2731 One caveat the bullish case tends to omit: car sales actually fell in 2024 from the 2022 record of 508,547, as affordability and credit conditions bit. The motorisation curve is real over a decade; it is not a smooth annual climb.31

Two wheels: the bigger market, electrified rather than retired

Motorbikes are not a legacy category to be managed down. Vietnam is the world's fourth-largest motorcycle market and the third-largest for electric two-wheelers; after a soft 2024 it rebounded to around 3.4 million units in 2025, with Honda at roughly 80%.29 The decreed intent, in Decision 876 and Decision 2530, is to convert that fleet to electric, not shrink it: an end to fossil-fuel two-wheeler manufacture and import by 2040, and 100% of motorcycles electric by 2050, with Hanoi's first hard deadline landing in 2026.252616 Demand-side incentives make the switch cheap: battery EVs carry a 100% registration-fee exemption to February 2027 and a special-consumption-tax rate as low as 3% against 35 to 50% for combustion cars.12 The clearest evidence the policy works is concentration in one champion: VinFast took an estimated 36% of the 2025 car market, which a foreign entrant should read as a signal that demand is real and the finished-vehicle slot is taken.14

04 · Opening twoLocalisation: the supplier gap the state is paying to close

The weakest link in Vietnam's automotive story is also the cleanest foreign opening: the domestic supplier base. Passenger-car localisation sits at only 7 to 20%, with roughly 80% of components imported, an import pool of an estimated $2 to 3.5 billion a year.33 The country has fewer than 100 Tier-1 suppliers and around 150 lower-tier suppliers, against Thailand's roughly 690 and 1,700 built over four decades.33 That contrast is both the deficit and the runway.

The supplier targets sit in approved instruments. Resolution 115/NQ-CP (2020) aimed for 1,000 supplier enterprises serving assemblers and multinationals by 2025, doubling to about 2,000 by 2030, with supporting-industry products meeting 70% of domestic demand by 2030.32 The money sits in Decree 111/2015, as amended by Decree 205/2025 (effective September 2025), which offers collaborative projects up to 50% of qualifying investment cost, stacking with Decree 182 for automotive-grade electronics.329 The pull is already visible: Toyota works with around 60 local suppliers, Hyundai Thanh Cong is sourcing for a high-localisation 2027 model, Thaco runs fifteen component factories, and VinFast reports above 60% localisation with a battery joint venture with Gotion in Ha Tinh.35 Crucially, the electric transition resets the parts mix toward batteries, motors and electronics, where no entrenched Vietnamese incumbent yet exists.

Thailand built its supplier base over forty years around combustion engines. Vietnam is trying to build its base in ten, around batteries and electronics, with cash on the table. That is the opening, and the reason to qualify early.

The base today is back-end: assembly, packaging and testing, the lower-margin end. The instruments in Sections 03, 04 and 05 are parts of the same system: Vietnam cannot electrify its vehicle fleet or build its components base at the decreed pace without the semiconductor and electronics supply chain sitting behind it.

06 · The reality checkWhat could break it, and how to read the targets

The instruments describe intent. Two things determine whether the intent becomes outcome: power, and Vietnam's delivery record.

Power is the binding constraint

Electrifying millions of vehicles and running AI data centres rests on the grid, and that is itself a decree: the Revised Power Development Plan 8 (Decision 768/QD-TTg, April 2025) targets roughly 90 to 100 GW of capacity by 2030, renewables at 28 to 36% of the mix, battery storage scaled from 300 MW to 10,000 to 16,300 MW, and the reintroduction of nuclear, requiring about $136 billion of investment this decade.37 The risk is delivery, not ambition: transmission is already congested in the south and centre, renewable output has been curtailed, and project timelines slip, which is why the state issued a further energy-security resolution in August 2025. If the grid lags, the EV and data-centre theses both slow. This is the single dependency a foreign entrant should diligence first.37

Read the decreed targets against the record

Vietnam writes ambitious numbers and frequently misses them. The previous auto-localisation plan targeted 40 to 45% by 2025 and delivered 7 to 20%; Resolution 115 sought 1,000 component suppliers by 2025 and the country has fewer than 100 at Tier-1; the auto-market target was cut between drafts; and the 2026 to 2030 double-digit GDP goal is widely judged unlikely. None of this negates the openings. It means the right posture is to back the direction the instruments set, while discounting the magnitude and timing they promise.

And the residual risks

Transition disruption. Merging ministries and provinces while standing up new bodies creates licensing-continuity risk during the handover.3
Transshipment enforcement is undefined. The 40% penalty turns on how “substantial transformation” is interpreted, and the rules were not fully specified at the time of writing.5
The talent market is tight. The 50,000-engineer semiconductor goal runs against a workforce officials concede is short on R&D depth; localisation plans should assume expensive engineering.18

The distinctive feature of Vietnam's current model is the degree to which the government has published its intentions in advance, dated them, and priced them in legislation and budget. For a senior executive that transparency is both an advantage and a responsibility: an advantage because the direction is unusually legible; a responsibility because acting on it requires distinguishing between what the instruments commit to and what the country can actually deliver on the stated timetable.

The instruments in this briefing tell a consistent story. Vietnam is attempting to move from assembly to manufacturing, from combustion to electric, from imported components to a local supply chain, and from a low-cost labour platform to a technology-capable industrial economy. Whether it succeeds fully, partially or more slowly than planned, the instruments define where effort and capital are being directed. For a foreign company considering the region, that is the actionable information.

The targets may change. The timelines will slip. The direction, in our assessment, generally holds.

The instruments should not be read as forecasts. They should be read as signals.
